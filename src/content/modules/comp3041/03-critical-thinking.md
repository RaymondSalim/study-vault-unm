## Critical Thinking

### Definition (Kurfiss, 1988)
A rational response to questions that cannot be answered definitively and for which all relevant knowledge may not be available. It integrates all available information and can be convincingly justified.

### Key Characteristics
- Self-directed, self-disciplined, self-monitored, self-corrective
- Requires rigorous standards of excellence
- All assumptions are open to question
- Divergent views are aggressively sought
- Inquiry is not biased toward a particular outcome

## What is a Fallacy?

A fallacy is a **defect in an argument** where the conclusion is not supported by the propositions.

| Type | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Formal fallacy** | Flawed due to error in the structure of the argument |
| **Informal fallacy** | Flawed due to error in the content of the argument |

### Three Categories of Informal Fallacies

| Category | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Fallacies of Relevance** | Premises are logically irrelevant to conclusion (appeal to psychological/emotional relevance) |
| **Fallacies of Ambiguity** | Relies on vagueness/obscurity; meanings shift during the argument |
| **Fallacies of Presumption** | Rests on hidden assumptions that don't support the conclusion |

## Fallacies of Relevance (Red Herring Fallacies)

| Fallacy | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Ad hominem (abusive)** | Attacking the person instead of the argument | "You're a rule breaker, why should I listen to your ethics arguments?" |
| **Ad hominem (circumstantial)** | Discrediting arguer by implying ulterior motives | "Prof argues for higher salaries because it helps him personally" |
| **Tu quoque** | "You too" — charging hypocrisy to dismiss argument | "You smoked at 16, so I won't listen to your anti-smoking advice" |
| **Straw man** | Misrepresenting someone's argument to make it easier to attack | "We should have stricter data privacy" → "So you want to shut down all tech companies?" |
| **Poisoning the well** | Pre-emptively discrediting opponent before they speak | "Maria will make some feeble effort to get out of this mess" |
| **Appeal to emotion** | Manipulating emotions instead of using valid reasoning | "You shouldn't break up with me because losing you hurts so much" |
| **Appeal to tradition** | Supporting conclusion solely because it's long been held true | "Vote Republican because your father and grandfather did" |
| **Appeal to consequences** | Truth determined by whether consequences are good/bad | "God doesn't exist — look at all the harm done in his name" |
| **Inappropriate appeal to authority** | Using a false authority to back assertion | "My maths professor lectured on atheism, he must be right" |
| **Association fallacy** | Guilt/praise by association rather than evidence | "Elon Musk supports this crypto, so it must be good" |

## Faulty Generalisation Fallacies

| Fallacy | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Hasty generalisation** | Broad conclusion from small sample | "8 of 10 doctors recommend X, therefore it must be good" |
| **Cherry picking** | Selecting only evidence that supports argument, ignoring contradictions | Resumes — only showing best achievements |
| **Misleading vividness** | Compelling by vivid/memorable evidence instead of reliable evidence | Refusing to fly after seeing one emergency landing |
| **False analogy** | Comparison that doesn't hold under scrutiny | "Renting is like flushing money down the toilet" |
| **Accident** | Applying a general rule to an exception | "Cutting people with knives is criminal → surgeons are criminals" |
| **Overwhelming exception** | So many exceptions the general statement loses value | "Our AI is unbiased, except for age, gender, or ethnicity" |

## Other Important Fallacies

| Fallacy | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Circular reasoning** | Conclusion restates the premise | "Abortion is wrong because it's unethical" |
| **Confirmation bias** | Only looking for evidence that confirms existing beliefs | Researcher only interviewing people who agree with them |
| **False cause** | Imagined causal connection (correlation ≠ causation) | "Ice cream sales rise with drowning → ice cream causes drowning" |
| **Bifurcation** | Only two choices presented when more exist | "You're either with us or against us" |
| **False dilemma** | Reducing complex issue to limited options | Menu: "vegetarian, vegan, or meat" — ignoring gluten-free, organic, etc. |
| **Fallacy of composition** | What's true of parts must be true of whole | "Every player is a superstar, so the team is unbeatable" |
| **Fallacy of division** | What's true of whole must be true of parts | "The company is rich, so every employee must be rich" |
| **Argument from silence** | Conclusion based on lack of contrary evidence | "We've never seen ETs, therefore they don't exist" |
| **Argument from repetition** | Repeating an assertion hoping it becomes persuasive | Saying the same thing in slightly different ways in an exam |

## How to Avoid Fallacies

1. Research thoroughly — understand your topic
2. Stay focused on the discussion topic
3. Address counterarguments
4. Use evidence and logic to link ideas
5. Avoid claiming results without evidence
6. Look at the problem from different points of view
7. Ask someone to review your work

## Computing-Relevant Fallacy Examples

| Fallacy | Computing Example |
|---------|-------------------|
| Hasty generalisation | "One hire from State Uni was bad → don't hire from State Uni" |
| Fallacy of composition | "Each module works perfectly → the system works perfectly" |
| Fallacy of division | "Our system is secure → every component is secure" |
| Bifurcation | "You're either a hacker or a victim" |
| Appeal to authority | "My CS professor says climate change is fake" (not their expertise) |
