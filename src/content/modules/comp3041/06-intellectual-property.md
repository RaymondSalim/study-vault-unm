## What is Intellectual Property?

:::eli10

Intellectual property is like owning an idea. If you invent something clever or create something original (a song, a story, a new gadget), the law says other people cannot just copy it without your permission. This way, inventors and creators can earn money from their hard work.

:::

:::eli15
Intellectual property (IP) refers to creations of the mind (inventions, artistic works, brand names, trade secrets) that are protected by law. Unlike physical property, ideas can be copied without "taking" them from the owner, so IP law exists to ensure creators can benefit from their work even after the idea becomes known. The four main types of IP protection are patents, trademarks, copyright, and trade secrets.

:::

:::eli20
Products of creativity and invention — different from tangible property.

The goal of IP law: ensure people with good ideas can profit from them, even after the idea is known.

:::

## Four Types of IP Protection

:::eli10

There are four ways to protect ideas: patents protect inventions (for about 20 years), trademarks protect brand names and logos (can be renewed forever), copyright protects creative works like books and music (for a very long time), and trade secrets protect hidden business information (as long as you keep it secret).

:::

:::eli15
IP law provides four distinct mechanisms: patents grant exclusive rights to inventions for around 20 years in exchange for public disclosure; trademarks protect brand identifiers and can be renewed indefinitely; copyright automatically protects original creative works for the author's lifetime plus 50-70 years; and trade secrets protect confidential business information with no time limit as long as secrecy is maintained. Each serves a different purpose and has different requirements.

:::

:::eli20
| Type | Protects | Duration | Key Feature |
|------|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Patents** | Inventions (processes, machines) | ~20 years | Must be novel, non-obvious, useful |
| **Trademarks** | Signs distinguishing goods/services | 10 years (renewable) | Brand identity |
| **Copyright** | Original works of authorship | Life + 50-70 years | Automatic upon creation |
| **Trade Secrets** | Confidential business information | Unlimited (while secret) | Must be actively kept secret |

:::

## Patents

:::eli10

A patent is like a deal with the government: you show everyone how your invention works, and in return, you get 20 years where only you can make money from it. If someone else wants to use your invention, they have to pay you. But after 20 years, anyone can use it.

:::

:::eli15
Patents grant inventors exclusive rights for approximately 20 years in exchange for publicly disclosing how the invention works. To be patentable, an invention must be novel (not previously known), non-obvious (not an obvious solution to experts), and useful (has practical application). Patents are territorial (you must file in each country) and controversial in software, where "patent trolls" acquire broad patents solely to extract licensing fees through lawsuits.

:::

:::eli20
### Requirements for Patentability
- **Novel**: Not previously invented
- **Non-obvious**: Solution not obvious to specialists
- **Useful**: Has practical application

### Key Characteristics
- Exclusive rights for limited period (~20 years)
- In exchange for **public disclosure** of the invention
- Third parties must pay a licence fee to use
- Territorial (must file in each country)

### Software and Patents
- Software itself is usually excluded from patents in many jurisdictions
- Components/methods may be patentable
- Controversial in computing (patent trolls, overly broad patents)

### Case: Amazon 1-Click Patent
- 1997: Amazon offered 1-Click purchasing
- 1999: Patent granted for the method
- 2000: Apple licensed the patent
- Controversial: Is "one-click purchasing" really a novel invention?

### Patent Trolls
- Entities that acquire patents solely to extract licensing fees through lawsuits
- Don't produce products themselves
- Burden of proof on defendant
- Fighting infringement is extremely expensive

:::

## Trademarks

:::eli10

A trademark is a special name, logo, or symbol that tells you who made something. The Nike swoosh and the Apple apple are trademarks. They help you know which company made a product so you can choose the ones you trust.

:::

:::eli15
Trademarks are signs (words, logos, colours, sounds) that distinguish one company's products from another's. They serve multiple functions: identifying origin, helping consumer choice, signalling quality, enabling marketing, and creating economic value. Trademarks can be registered for 10-year renewable periods and give the owner exclusive rights to use the mark in trade, with legal remedies for infringement.

:::

:::eli20
### Definition
A sign capable of distinguishing goods/services of one enterprise from others.

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Trademark** | Distinguishes one enterprise's goods from others |
| **Collective mark** | Distinguishes goods of association members |
| **Certification mark** | Certifies origin, quality, accuracy |

### Functions
- **Origin**: Identifies source of products/services
- **Choice**: Helps consumers choose with ease
- **Quality**: Consumers associate with known quality
- **Marketing**: Promotes through advertising
- **Economic**: Valuable asset; can be licensed/franchised

### Registration (Malaysia)
- Exclusive right to use mark in trading
- Legal action for infringement
- Valid 10 years from application date (renewable)

:::

## Copyright

:::eli10

Copyright means that when you create something original, like a story, a drawing, or a song, it automatically belongs to you. Nobody else can copy it, share it, or change it without your permission. But there are some exceptions: teachers can use small pieces for teaching, and you can quote a little bit in a review.

:::

:::eli15
Copyright automatically protects original works of authorship (literature, music, art, software code) from the moment of creation. It gives the creator four exclusive rights: reproduction, derivative works, distribution, and public performance/display. Copyright does not protect ideas themselves, only their expression. "Fair use" allows limited reproduction for education, research, criticism, and commentary, judged by four factors including purpose, amount used, and market effect.

:::

:::eli20
### What it Covers
Original works of authorship in: literature, music, drama, graphic art, sculpture, motion pictures, sound recordings, architecture.

### Four Exclusive Rights
1. Right to **reproduce** the work
2. Right to prepare **derivative works**
3. Right to **distribute** copies
4. Right to **perform or display** publicly

### What Copyright Does NOT Cover
- Ideas, facts, or common knowledge
- Works not in tangible fixed form (e.g., unrecorded dance)

### Fair Use

Allows reproduction without permission for scholarship, teaching, research, criticism, commentary, news reporting.

**Four Factors** (all must be considered):
1. Purpose and character of use (commercial vs non-profit educational)
2. Nature of the copyrighted work
3. Amount and substantiality used relative to whole
4. Effect on potential market or value

### Fair Use Examples

| Scenario | Fair Use? | Reasoning |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Professor prints 2 chapters (35 pages each) for class | Debatable | Educational purpose, but substantial amount, market effect |
| Professor puts chapters on public web page | Likely NOT | Public access affects market |
| 10% from several books on password-protected site | More likely | Limited amount, restricted access, educational |

### Doctrine of First Sale
- Buy a physical copy → you own that copy
- Can resell, lend, or give away the physical object
- Copyright owner's control ends after first sale
- **Digital challenge**: "used" digital files are perfect copies — undermines creators' income

:::

## Trade Secrets

:::eli10

A trade secret is a secret recipe or special method that a company keeps hidden to stay ahead of competitors. Think of the Coca-Cola recipe. As long as you keep it secret, it is protected. But if someone figures it out on their own, you cannot stop them.

:::

:::eli15
Trade secrets are confidential business information that derives commercial value from being secret (formulas, algorithms, customer lists, manufacturing processes). Unlike patents, they require no registration and have no time limit, but they are only protected as long as the owner takes reasonable steps to maintain secrecy. If the secret is independently discovered or reverse-engineered, protection is lost.

:::

:::eli20
- Confidential information with commercial value
- Can be sold or licensed
- Unauthorised acquisition/disclosure is unfair practice
- No registration needed — protected as long as kept secret

:::

## IP Protection in Malaysia

:::eli10

Malaysia has different laws to protect different types of ideas. There are laws for inventions, brand names, creative works, product designs, computer chip designs, and even special regional products. Malaysia also works with international groups to protect ideas across countries.

:::

:::eli15
Malaysia provides comprehensive IP protection through multiple laws: the Patents Act 1983, Trade Marks Act 1976, Copyright Act 1987, Industrial Designs Act 1996, Layout-Designs of Integrated Circuits Act 2000, and Geographical Indications Act 2000. As a member of WIPO and signatory to major international treaties (Paris Convention, Berne Convention, TRIPS), Malaysia participates in the global IP protection system.

:::

:::eli20
| Law | Coverage |
|-----|----------|
| Patents Act 1983 | Inventions |
| Trade Marks Act 1976 | Brand identity |
| Copyright Act 1987 | Creative works |
| Industrial Designs Act 1996 | Product appearance |
| Layout-Designs of Integrated Circuits Act 2000 | Chip designs |
| Geographical Indications Act 2000 | Regional products |

Malaysia is a member of WIPO and signatory to Paris Convention, Berne Convention, and TRIPS (WTO).

:::
