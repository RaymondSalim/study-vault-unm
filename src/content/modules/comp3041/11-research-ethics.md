## Why Research Ethics?

:::eli10

Research ethics is about being honest and fair when doing science. It is not just about evil scientists doing bad things; it is about regular people making mistakes, taking shortcuts, or not thinking carefully enough. When researchers cheat, everyone loses because we cannot trust their results.

:::

:::eli15
Research ethics addresses misconduct that ranges from outright fraud to subtle shortcuts. It is not only about "bad people" but about imperfect people under pressure (publish-or-perish, tenure, grants) who may cut corners, deceive themselves, or find themselves in situations where the right course is unclear. Socially accepted practices may later be deemed wrong, and involvement in others' misconduct can also raise ethical issues.

:::

:::eli20
Research ethics isn't just about bad people doing bad things — it's about **imperfect people doing imperfect things** for various reasons:
- Socially acceptable practices later deemed wrong
- Accidents and shortcuts
- Self-deception and psychological tendencies
- Being involved through others' wrongs
- Situations where the right thing isn't clear

### Professional Pressures That Encourage Misconduct
- Publish or perish
- Tenure/retaining a job
- Keeping up with peers
- Securing grants
- Being first to a discovery

:::

## Moral Foundations of Research

:::eli10

The goal of research is to find the truth so we can help people, animals, and the planet. Researchers owe honesty to everyone: the public who pays for research, the people who participate in experiments, their coworkers, and the organisations that fund them.

:::

:::eli15
Research is morally grounded in the pursuit of truth and knowledge for the benefit of humanity, animals, and the environment. Researchers owe truthfulness to multiple stakeholders: the public (who funds research through taxes), participants (whose autonomy must be respected), colleagues (whose work builds on published findings), funders (who provide resources), and institutions (whose reputations are at stake). "The truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth" means being honest, not omitting inconvenient findings, and not overstating significance.

:::

:::eli20
- Doing good for humans, animals, the planet, future generations via pursuit of **truth and knowledge**
- Duty to respect individuals
- Possible duties to animals
- Obligations to society

### "The Truth, The Whole Truth, and Nothing But The Truth"

| Principle | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| The truth | Be honest about your research |
| The whole truth | Don't omit findings (may constitute misconduct) |
| Nothing but the truth | Don't add irrelevant/misleading information or overstate significance |

### To Whom Do We Owe the Truth?
- The public (for funding support)
- Research participants (respect for autonomy)
- Colleagues and collaborators (whose work builds on ours)
- Funding institutions (for resources given)
- Employers/universities (whose reputations we affect)

:::

## Research Misconduct

:::eli10

The three biggest research crimes are: fabrication (making up data that does not exist), falsification (changing real data to make it look better), and plagiarism (copying someone else's work and pretending it is yours). These are not honest mistakes; they are serious cheating that can get you fired or banned.

:::

:::eli15
Research misconduct is formally defined as fabrication (inventing data), falsification (manipulating data or processes to misrepresent findings), or plagiarism (taking others' ideas or words without credit). It does not include honest error or differences of opinion. Other forms include image manipulation, selective data suppression, and sabotaging competitors' work. Misconduct damages the integrity of the scientific record and can have real-world consequences when flawed findings inform policy or practice.

:::

:::eli20
### NSF Definition
Fabrication, falsification, or plagiarism in proposing, performing, reviewing, or reporting research. **Does not include** honest error or differences of opinion.

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Fabrication** | Making up data or results | Inventing experimental results |
| **Falsification** | Manipulating materials, processes, or data to misrepresent research | Image manipulation, data omission |
| **Plagiarism** | Appropriating another's ideas/results/words without credit | Copying text, stealing ideas from peer review |

### Other Forms
- Image manipulation
- Data suppression/omission
- Sabotage of others' work

:::

## Collaboration Issues

:::eli10

When people work together on research, they sometimes fight about who gets credit. The rule is: if you helped think up the ideas, do the work, analyse the results, or write the paper, you deserve to be listed as an author. Just paying for it or washing the lab equipment is not enough.

:::

:::eli15
Collaboration raises ethical questions about authorship credit and data ownership. Authorship requires substantive intellectual contribution (designing experiments, conducting research, analysing data, or writing the manuscript). Simply providing funding, routine technical support, or administrative assistance does not qualify. Data ownership disputes arise when collaborators leave institutions. The DeepMind-NHS case shows how data sharing between organisations can violate ethical principles when patients are not adequately informed or asked for consent.

:::

:::eli20
### Authorship Credit
Who deserves authorship? Must have contributed substantively to:
- Design of experiments
- Execution of research
- Analysis and interpretation of data
- Writing of the manuscript

| Role | Authorship? | Reasoning |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| Lab chief (designed experiments, edited drafts) | Yes | Intellectual contribution |
| Graduate student (conducted experiments, wrote drafts) | Yes (often first author) | Primary researcher |
| Technician (trained student, did procedures) | Debatable | Technical vs intellectual contribution |
| Funding director (obtained grants) | Usually no | Financial ≠ intellectual contribution |
| Glassware washer | No | Routine support |

### Data Ownership and Management
- Who "owns" the data?
- Can lab materials be taken off-site?
- What documentation responsibilities exist?

### Case: Google DeepMind & NHS (2015)
- DeepMind given 1.6 million patient records for AI kidney injury app
- Patients not adequately informed or asked for consent
- ICO ruled the data-sharing violated UK data protection laws
- Ethical issues: autonomy, transparency, commercial vs healthcare benefit

:::

## Peer Review

:::eli10

Peer review is when other scientists check your work before it gets published. But this creates problems: what if a reviewer steals your idea? What if they use what they learned from your paper to get ahead? Reviewers must be honest and keep what they read secret.

:::

:::eli15
Peer review is essential for quality control in research, but it creates ethical challenges. Reviewers gain access to unpublished ideas and could use that information to advance their own research (a form of intellectual theft). There are questions about whether graduate students can review on behalf of professors and how to protect IP during the review process. The University of Minnesota Linux kernel case (2021) shows how researchers can abuse the peer review system itself: they submitted intentionally vulnerable code without IRB approval, violating the trust of the open-source community and getting their institution banned.

:::

:::eli20
### Ethical Issues
- Using information learned from reviewing to advance own research
- Protecting intellectual property during review
- Whether graduate students can review on behalf of professors

### Case: University of Minnesota & Linux Kernel (2021)
- Researchers submitted intentionally vulnerable "hypocrite commits" to Linux kernel
- Tested if code review process would catch them
- **No IRB approval** obtained; trust of community violated
- **Outcome**: University banned from Linux kernel contributions; comprehensive review of past submissions

:::

## Conflicts of Interest & Obligation

:::eli10

A conflict of interest is when what is good for you personally clashes with what you should do in your job. For example, if a company pays for your research, you might feel pressure to say nice things about them even if your results are not so positive. Honest researchers declare these conflicts so people know about potential bias.

:::

:::eli15
Conflicts of interest arise when personal interests (financial gain, career advancement) conflict with professional obligations (truthful reporting, unbiased research). They can be direct (paid to say something), indirect (hoping positive findings lead to future funding), or involve publication control (funders restricting what can be published). Conflicts of obligation occur when duties to multiple parties (research, teaching, family) compete. Additionally, the source of funding raises moral questions about complicity, especially for dual-use or weaponisable research.

:::

:::eli20
### Conflict of Interest
A situation where personal interests conflict with professional obligations.

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| Direct | Being paid to say something untrue |
| Indirect | Knowing positive statements about a funder may lead to future grants |
| Publication control | Funder stipulates right to delay/prevent publication |

### Conflicts of Obligation
Having duties to multiple parties simultaneously (e.g., research vs teaching vs family).

### Complicity and Funding
Moral issues beyond misconduct depending on research field and funding source:
- Stem cell research (human embryos)
- Dual-use biological agents
- Weaponisable technology / defence funding

:::

## Animal Subject Research: The "3 Rs"

:::eli10

When scientists do experiments on animals, they must follow three rules: make the experiments less painful (refine), use fewer animals (reduce), and try to use simpler creatures instead of complex ones (replace). The goal is to hurt as few animals as little as possible.

:::

:::eli15
Ethical animal research follows the "3 Rs" framework. Refine: design experiments to minimise pain and distress. Reduce: use the minimum number of animals necessary for valid results. Replace: where possible, use lower-order organisms, cell cultures, or computer simulations instead of higher-order animals. These principles balance the potential benefits of research against the moral obligation to minimise animal suffering.

:::

:::eli20
| Principle | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Refine** | Refine experiments to cause less pain and distress |
| **Reduce** | Reduce number of animals used |
| **Replace** | Replace higher-order animals with lower-order ones |

:::

## Human Subject Research

:::eli10

When scientists do experiments involving people, the most important rule is that people must freely choose to participate and understand what will happen. Nobody can be forced. The experiment must not cause unnecessary pain. And if things get dangerous, the scientist must stop immediately.

:::

:::eli15
Human subject research is governed by the Nuremberg Code (created after Nazi medical experiments). Its key principles include: voluntary informed consent is absolutely essential, experiments must be scientifically justified and not achievable by other means, all unnecessary suffering must be avoided, risk must not exceed the humanitarian importance of the problem, and subjects can withdraw at any time. These principles form the foundation of modern research ethics boards (IRBs) that must approve any research involving humans.

:::

:::eli20
### Nuremberg Code (Key Principles)
1. **Voluntary informed consent** is absolutely essential
2. Experiment should yield fruitful results unprocurable by other means
3. Should be based on prior animal experimentation
4. Must avoid all unnecessary physical/mental suffering
5. No experiment where death/disabling injury is expected
6. Risk must not exceed humanitarian importance
7. Proper preparations to protect against injury
8. Only scientifically qualified persons may conduct
9. Subject may end participation at any time
10. Scientist must be prepared to terminate if probable cause of harm

:::

## The Responsible Computing Research Framework

:::eli10

When doing computing research, follow three steps: plan honestly (propose), design it to be ethical from the start (design), and build it carefully with high standards (execute). Ethics should be part of the plan from day one, not an afterthought.

:::

:::eli15
The Responsible Computing Research Framework provides guidance for ethical research across three phases: PROPOSE (plan with integrity, identify ethical risks early), DESIGN (architect for ethics, build in safeguards and consent mechanisms), and EXECUTE (build with rigour, follow approved protocols, handle data responsibly). This framework ensures ethics is integrated throughout the research process rather than treated as a compliance checkbox.

:::

:::eli20
| Phase | Principle |
|-------|-----------|
| PROPOSE | Plan with integrity |
| DESIGN | Architect for ethics |
| EXECUTE | Build with rigour |

:::
