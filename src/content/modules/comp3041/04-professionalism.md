## What is a Profession?

:::eli10

A profession is a special job where you need a lot of training and you promise to help people, not hurt them. Doctors, lawyers, and engineers are all professionals. Being a professional means following extra rules to keep people safe and trusting you.

:::

:::eli15

A profession is more than just a job. It requires specialised knowledge gained through extensive education, adherence to a code of ethics, and a commitment to serving the public interest. Professions typically have certification or licensing requirements, professional associations, and mechanisms for holding members accountable. Computing is still debating whether it fully qualifies as a profession in this traditional sense.

:::

:::eli20

> "A disciplined group of individuals who adhere to high ethical standards and uphold themselves to, and are accepted by, the public as possessing special knowledge and skills in a widely recognised, organised body of learning derived from education and training at a high level, and who are prepared to exercise this knowledge and these skills in the interest of others." — Australian Council of Professions

### Kultgen's Attributes of Professions

A fully developed profession has:
- Specialised knowledge and training
- Code of ethics
- Certification/licensing process
- Professional associations
- Service orientation (public interest)

:::

## Path to Entering a Profession

:::eli10

To become a professional, you go through steps like going to school, passing tests, getting a certificate or licence, joining a group of other professionals, and promising to keep learning. It is like levels in a video game, where each level makes you more trusted.

:::

:::eli15

The professional pathway typically involves: formal education at an accredited institution, certification (voluntary proof of competence) or licensing (mandatory government permission to practice), membership in a professional society, commitment to a code of ethics, and ongoing professional development. In computing, certifications like CCNA or AWS Solutions Architect demonstrate specialist competence, though most jurisdictions do not require a licence to write software.

:::

:::eli20

```
Education → Accreditation → Certification → Licensing → Professional Society → Code of Ethics → Continuing Development
```

### Certification vs Licensing

| Aspect | Certification | Licensing |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| Nature | Voluntary | Non-voluntary (mandatory) |
| Granted by | Non-governmental professional organisation | Government agency |
| Purpose | Attests mastery of specific knowledge/skills | Grants permission to practice |
| Consequence | Recognition of competence | Illegal to practice without one |

### Computing Certifications

| Certification | Focus |
|--------------|-------|
| Cisco CCNA | Network fundamentals |
| Certified Ethical Hacker | Security testing techniques |
| Google Professional Data Engineer | Cloud data systems |
| AWS Solutions Architect | Cloud architecture design |
| BCS (British Computer Society) | Various IT certifications |

:::

## Licensed Software Engineers

:::eli10

Some software engineers need a special licence, just like a doctor needs one. This is for people who build systems where a bug could hurt or even kill people, like software for aeroplanes or hospitals.

:::

:::eli15

Software engineering licensing is required when working on safety-critical systems whose failure could endanger the public (medical devices, aviation, infrastructure). Licensed engineers must hold a degree, pass professional exams, demonstrate work experience, maintain good moral character, and continue their education. This is more common in traditional engineering fields, but the debate is growing as software becomes more safety-critical.

:::

:::eli20

Required when working on systems whose failure could affect the public.

Requirements: Degree + Engineering exam + Work experience + Good moral character + Continuing education + Principles and practice exam

:::

## Professional Associations

:::eli10

Professional associations are clubs for people in the same profession. Members help each other, share knowledge, and agree on rules for doing good work. The biggest ones for computing are IEEE and ACM.

:::

:::eli15

Professional associations like IEEE and ACM bring computing professionals together, set ethical standards, publish research, and advocate for the profession. They provide codes of conduct, organise conferences, and offer resources for professional development. Membership signals commitment to professional standards and ongoing learning.

:::

:::eli20

| Organisation | Members | Focus |
|-------------|---------|-------|
| IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronic Engineers) | 420,000+ in 160+ countries | Electrical/electronic/computing engineering |
| ACM (Association for Computing Machinery) | 100,000+ | Computing as a science and profession |
| MNCC (Malaysian National Computer Confederation) | Malaysia-based | Computing professionals in Malaysia |

:::

## The SE Code of Ethics and Professional Practice

:::eli10

Software engineers have a set of eight promises they make. The most important promise is to always put the public (regular people) first. Other promises include doing good work, being honest, being fair to coworkers, and always learning new things.

:::

:::eli15

The Software Engineering Code of Ethics has eight principles covering obligations to the public, clients, product quality, professional judgment, management, the profession itself, colleagues, and self-development. The public interest is paramount: if your employer asks you to do something that could harm users, the code says you should prioritise public safety. The code should be read as a whole rather than cherry-picking individual clauses.

:::

:::eli20

### Preamble
- Software engineers have opportunities to do good or harm
- Should be committed to doing good
- Eight principles identify key ethical relationships
- Code should be seen as a **whole**, not a collection of parts
- Public interest is **paramount**

### Eight Principles

| # | Principle | Focus |
|---|-----------|-------|
| 1 | **Public Interest** | Act consistently with the public interest |
| 2 | **Client & Employer** | Act in best interests of client/employer, consistent with public interest |
| 3 | **Product** | Ensure product meets highest professional standards |
| 4 | **Judgment** | Maintain integrity and independence in professional judgment |
| 5 | **Management** | Promote ethical approach to software management |
| 6 | **Profession** | Advance integrity and reputation of the profession |
| 7 | **Colleagues** | Be fair to and supportive of colleagues |
| 8 | **Self** | Participate in lifelong learning, promote ethical practice |

### Key Clauses (Selected)

| Clause | Content |
|--------|---------|
| 1.01 | Accept full responsibility for own work |
| 1.03 | Approve software only if it is safe |
| 1.04 | Disclose actual/potential dangers |
| 2.05 | Respect confidentiality |
| 2.09 | Put employer's/client's interests first unless overriding moral concern |
| 3.10 | Ensure adequate testing, debugging, and review |
| 4.05 | Disclose conflicts of interest |
| 5.12 | Do not punish anyone for expressing ethical concerns |
| 6.13 | "Blow the whistle" when no alternative to reporting significant violations |
| 8.07 | Do not treat others unfairly because of prejudices |

:::

## Alternative Fundamental Principles

:::eli10

There are also simpler rules professionals can follow: be fair, do not hide important information, respect other people's rights, take responsibility for your actions, share what you know, keep your promises, and always try to get better at your job.

:::

:::eli15

Beyond formal codes, several fundamental principles guide professional conduct: impartiality (balance public, company, and personal interests), disclosure (do not let others be harmed by hidden information), respect for rights (privacy, property), just treatment (fair wages, credit, no discrimination), responsibility (for actions and inactions), knowledge-sharing, integrity, continuous improvement, and responsible supervision.

:::

:::eli20

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Be impartial | Public good = company good = personal good |
| Disclose information | Don't let others come to harm by concealing information |
| Respect rights of others | Privacy, property, intellectual property |
| Treat others justly | Fair wages, appropriate credit, no discrimination |
| Take responsibility | For actions AND inactions |
| Share knowledge | Volunteer skills, help bring others up |
| Maintain integrity | Deliver commitments, obey law |
| Continually improve | Stay current, improve skills |
| Supervise responsibly | Set up training, monitor quality, maintain communication |

:::

## Applying the Code: Worked Example

:::eli10

Imagine Dan finds something illegal on a coworker's computer while doing his job. He should not have been looking at private files, but now he knows something bad. He has to tell someone in charge, but carefully and privately, because the coworker might not be guilty and Dan also broke a rule.

:::

:::eli15

In the worked example, Dan discovers illegal material on an employee's computer. The analysis involves multiple principles: Dan violated company privacy policy to find it, but now has knowledge of potential criminal activity. The code requires him to disclose the information to appropriate authorities while recognising that his own breach of policy was wrong and that the files alone do not prove guilt (someone else could have stored them). This shows how multiple principles interact in real scenarios.

:::

:::eli20

### Scenario: Dan finds illegal material on an employee's computer

**Step 1**: Identify relevant principles → Be impartial, Respect rights, Treat justly, Maintain integrity

**Step 2**: Examine relevant clauses:
- 2.03: Employee misused company equipment → obligation to report
- 2.09: Revealing images may harm employee (illegal possession)
- 3.13: Dan discovered material by violating company privacy policy
- 5.10: Files on computer ≠ guilt (could be stored by someone else)

**Conclusion**: Dan was wrong to violate policy, but once he has the knowledge, he is obliged to share it with company authorities — discreetly.

:::

## MNCC Code of Ethics (Malaysia)

:::eli10

In Malaysia, computing professionals have their own set of six promises: be honest and skilled, keep secrets when told them, give fair advice, take responsibility for your work, keep learning, and do not cheat to get ahead.

:::

:::eli15

The Malaysian National Computer Confederation (MNCC) Code of Ethics has six principles specific to Malaysian computing professionals: act with integrity and competence, maintain confidentiality, provide impartial advice while disclosing conflicts of interest, accept full responsibility for work undertaken, continuously develop professional knowledge, and conduct oneself properly without seeking personal advantage at the expense of the organisation.

:::

:::eli20

Six principles:
1. Act with **integrity** and competence
2. Act with **discretion** regarding confidential information
3. Act with **impartiality** when giving advice; disclose conflicts of interest
4. Accept full **responsibility** for work undertaken
5. Continuously enhance **professional knowledge**
6. Conduct self properly; not seek personal advantage to detriment of MNCC

:::
