## Key Definitions

:::eli10

Pervasive technology is technology that is everywhere around you (like smartphones, smart speakers, and cameras). Autonomous technology is technology that makes decisions by itself without a human telling it what to do (like a thermostat that decides when to turn the heat on). These two things often go together.

:::

:::eli15

Pervasive technology has spread so widely it becomes invisible and embedded in daily life (smartphones, IoT sensors, smart home devices). Autonomous technology operates independently without human intervention (self-driving cars, algorithmic trading). These are distinct concepts: autonomy does not require AI (a thermostat is autonomous), a robot is not necessarily autonomous (remote-controlled drones), and AI is not necessarily embodied (ChatGPT has no physical form). Understanding these distinctions prevents confused ethical analysis.

:::

:::eli20

| Concept | Definition | Key Feature |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Pervasive Technology** | Technology that has spread widely throughout society | Ubiquitous, often invisible, embedded in daily life |
| **Autonomous Technology** | Systems that operate independently of human intervention | Decision-making freedom without outside constraints |

### Important Distinctions

| Misconception | Reality |
|---------------|---------|
| Autonomy = Intelligence | Autonomous systems don't need AI |
| Autonomy = Robot | Not all autonomous tech has a physical body |
| Robot = Autonomous + AI | These are three separate concepts |

### Clarifying Three Concepts

| Concept | Definition | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Robot** | Electro-mechanical device capable of movement/action | Manufacturing arm, vacuum robot |
| **Artificial Intelligence** | Human-made artifact with ability to learn and reason | ChatGPT, image recognition |
| **Autonomous Technology** | Can operate without human intervention | Thermostat, autopilot |

:::

## The Autonomy–Pervasiveness Cycle

:::eli10

When technology can work on its own (autonomy), it becomes cheaper because you do not need humans to run it, so more people can afford it and it spreads everywhere (pervasiveness). And when technology is everywhere, companies make enough money to invest in making it even more independent. They feed each other in a cycle.

:::

:::eli15

Autonomy and pervasiveness reinforce each other in a cycle. Greater autonomy reduces operational costs (fewer human operators needed), making technology more affordable and thus more widely adopted (pervasive). Conversely, pervasiveness creates large user bases and revenue, justifying investment in further automation. Example: chatbots replaced expensive call centres, making 24/7 support affordable for small businesses (autonomy drove pervasiveness). Once everyone had smartphones, companies invested in AI assistants like Siri (pervasiveness drove autonomy).

:::

:::eli20

### Path 1: Autonomy → Pervasiveness
- Greater autonomy → cheaper to use
- Fewer human operators → lower costs → more affordable → wider adoption
- **Example**: Chatbots replaced expensive call centres, making 24/7 support affordable for small businesses

### Path 2: Pervasiveness → Autonomy
- Large user base → more revenue potential
- Justify automation investment → increased autonomy
- **Example**: Once everyone had smartphones, companies invested in AI assistants (Siri, Google Assistant)

:::

## Convergence in Action

:::eli10

Self-driving cars talk to smart traffic lights. Smart homes control temperature and lights by themselves while sensors watch every room. Social media algorithms decide what you see without any human choosing. All of these combine autonomous decisions with technology that is everywhere around you.

:::

:::eli15

The convergence of autonomy and pervasiveness creates powerful systems across many domains. Autonomous vehicles communicate with IoT infrastructure (smart traffic lights, road sensors). Smart homes combine autonomous climate/lighting control with pervasive monitoring. Social media algorithms autonomously curate content for billions of users, creating self-reinforcing feedback loops. Predictive maintenance uses pervasive sensor data for autonomous diagnosis. Each combination amplifies both the benefits and the ethical risks.

:::

:::eli20

| Domain | How Autonomy + Pervasiveness Combine |
|--------|--------------------------------------|
| Autonomous Vehicles + IoT | Self-driving cars communicate with smart traffic lights; sensors optimise routes |
| Smart Homes | Autonomous temperature/lighting + pervasive monitoring across all rooms |
| Social Media Algorithms | Autonomous content curation + pervasive presence (billions of users); self-reinforcing |
| Predictive Maintenance | Autonomous diagnosis + pervasive data collection from all connected devices |

:::

## Ethical Issues

:::eli10

Camera phones mean everyone can be recorded at any time. GPS can give bad directions that hurt people. Lifelogging (recording everything all the time) helps with memory but invades everyone else's privacy. When technology is everywhere and makes decisions on its own, we have to ask: who is responsible when things go wrong?

:::

:::eli15

Pervasive and autonomous technologies raise unique ethical challenges. Camera phones transformed privacy (everyone carries multiple cameras). GPS cases raise questions about responsibility when human-computer collaboration produces bad outcomes (who is to blame when someone follows GPS directions onto a dangerous highway?). Lifelogging (continuous audio/video recording) offers benefits (memory aid, accountability) but creates constant surveillance of bystanders. The core ethical question is: who bears responsibility when autonomous systems embedded everywhere cause harm?

:::

:::eli20

### Case: Pervasiveness of Camera Phones
- Camera Phone Predator Alert Act (2009, proposed)
- Every person may carry 7+ cameras daily
- Changed relationship with photography and privacy
- **Lifelogging**: Recording every moment (audio + video, never turned off)
  - Benefits: Memory aid, accountability, evidence
  - Dangers: Constant surveillance, privacy of others, data security

### Case: Injured by GPS (2010)
- Lauren Rosenberg followed Google Maps walking directions onto a highway with no sidewalks
- Hit by car; sued Google for negligent directions
- **Question**: Who is responsible when human + computer collaborate on a decision with bad outcome?

| Party | Argument for Responsibility |
|-------|---------------------------|
| Google | Provided directions without adequate safety warnings |
| The user | Should have used common sense; saw it was a highway |
| The driver | Hit a pedestrian (negligent driving) |

### Key Ethical Questions

| Question | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------------|
| Who is responsible when autonomous systems cause harm? | Gap between traditional liability and autonomous action |
| Should autonomous systems make life/death decisions? | Self-driving car dilemmas |
| What happens when pervasive tech creates surveillance? | Power imbalance between collectors and subjects |
| How do we maintain human agency? | Dependence on autonomous systems may erode autonomy |
| Who controls the algorithms? | Opacity of decision-making in pervasive systems |

:::

## Ethical Framework Application

:::eli10

When deciding if a new autonomous technology (like a self-driving bus on campus) is ethical, you can look at it from different angles: will it help or hurt students (consequences)? Is the university being responsible (virtue)? Does the university have a duty to keep people safe (rules)? Did they test it enough (professional codes)?

:::

:::eli15

Applying ethical frameworks to autonomous/pervasive systems requires considering multiple stakeholders. For a self-driving bus on campus: consequentialism evaluates outcomes for students (safety, convenience), virtue ethics asks if the innovation is responsibly deployed, deontology examines the duty of care owed to people on campus, and professional codes demand adequate testing. Five key questions should be asked of any such system: is it reliable, safe, accountable, transparent, and consensual?

:::

:::eli20

### Self-Driving Buses on Campus (Discussion Scenario)

| Stakeholder | Concern | Framework |
|-------------|---------|-----------|
| Students | Personal safety, reliability | **Consequentialism**: Outcomes for users |
| Staff | Job displacement, trust | **Virtue Ethics**: Responsible innovation |
| Administrators | Liability, cost-benefit | **Deontology**: Duty of care |
| Safety advocates | Fail-safe design, testing | **Professional codes**: Ensure adequate testing |

### Questions to Ask About Any Autonomous/Pervasive System:

1. **Reliability**: How does it guarantee consistent operation without unexpected errors?
2. **Safety**: How does it minimise risk to people who use or encounter it?
3. **Accountability**: Who is responsible when something goes wrong?
4. **Transparency**: Can users understand how decisions are made?
5. **Consent**: Have affected people agreed to interact with this system?

:::
