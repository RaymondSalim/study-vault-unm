## Why Ethics Matters in Computing

:::eli10

Imagine you build a robot that can do things for people. Ethics is about making sure your robot helps people and does not hurt them. When you write computer programs, they affect real people, so you need to think about what is fair and kind.

:::

:::eli15
Ethics in computing means thinking carefully about the impact of the software and systems we build. Since technology touches almost every part of life (healthcare, finance, social media), developers have a responsibility to consider whether their work could harm people, violate their rights, or be used unfairly. This course provides tools for navigating those tough decisions.

:::

:::eli20
### Ethics Defined

Ethics is a branch of philosophy addressing questions of morality:
- What is morally right and wrong?
- What is morally good and bad?
- What is morally just and unjust?

### What This Course Is

| It is | It is not |
|-------|-----------|
| A forum for discussion and debate | A philosophy class with all the answers |
| A toolkit for navigating tough professional choices | A lecture on "right" vs. "wrong" |
| An exploration of impact, consequences, and responsibility | About making you feel guilty |

:::

## General vs Professional Ethics

:::eli10

General ethics is like the basic rules everyone follows (be kind, do not steal). Professional ethics is like extra rules for people with special jobs. A doctor has rules about patients, and a computer programmer has rules about how to handle people's data safely.

:::

:::eli15
General ethics covers broad moral principles that apply to everyone. Professional ethics adds a layer specific to a profession: doctors must protect patient health, lawyers must protect client confidentiality, and computing professionals must protect users and the public from the harms their systems can cause. Without professional ethics, developers might not recognise when their everyday work creates ethical problems.

:::

:::eli20
| Level | Scope |
|-------|-------|
| **General Ethics** | Foundation for understanding moral principles |
| **Professional Ethics** | Specific to each profession (medical, engineering, computing) |

### Why Professional Ethics?

- We may not recognise ethical issues in everyday practice
- We may automatically do the "easy" thing
- We may do things we subsequently regret
- We may get ourselves and our employer into trouble
- We may miss opportunities to do good

:::

## The Power of Code

:::eli10

When you write a computer program, it is like writing rules that millions of people have to follow. If your rules are unfair, millions of people get treated unfairly. That is why coding is a superpower that comes with responsibility.

:::

:::eli15
Software is not neutral. Every design choice (what data to collect, how to rank content, who gets approved for a loan) has real consequences for people's lives. Because software now touches healthcare, finance, employment, and public discourse, a single algorithmic decision can affect millions. Developers wield enormous invisible power.

:::

:::eli20
- Code is **not neutral** — it allocates resources, makes choices, influences behaviour
- Software decisions affect: people's lives, livelihoods, public discourse, fundamental rights
- "Software is eating the world" — Marc Andreessen

### Computing's Pervasive Impact

| Domain | Example |
|--------|---------|
| Health services | Medical devices, diagnostic systems |
| Control systems | Engines, machinery, autonomous vehicles |
| Finance | Trading algorithms, banking |
| Decisions | Algorithmic hiring, loan applications |

:::

## Ethical Dilemmas

:::eli10

A dilemma is when two good things fight each other. For example, you want to be a loyal friend, but your friend asks you to help them cheat on a test. You cannot do both the right things at once, so you have to choose.

:::

:::eli15
An ethical dilemma occurs when two values you care about come into conflict and you cannot satisfy both. In computing, this happens constantly: user privacy vs. business profit, free speech vs. preventing harm, loyalty to your employer vs. reporting wrongdoing. Recognising these tensions is the first step toward resolving them thoughtfully.

:::

:::eli20
A dilemma arises when competing values conflict:

| Scenario | Tension |
|----------|---------|
| Expressing opinions in a group project | Freedom of expression vs sensitivity |
| Friend asks you to help them cheat | Loyalty vs academic integrity |
| Discovering online bullying | Personal safety vs responsibility to act |
| Using AI-generated essay | Convenience vs honesty |

:::

## Key Case Study: Cambridge Analytica

:::eli10

Facebook let a company called Cambridge Analytica look at millions of people's private information without asking them. That company used the information to try to change how people vote. It was like reading someone's diary and using their secrets against them.

:::

:::eli15
Cambridge Analytica harvested data from millions of Facebook users without their meaningful consent and used it to build psychological profiles for political micro-targeting. This case shows the tension between a data-driven business model (which profits from collecting user data) and the ethical obligation to protect user privacy and autonomy. It led to global regulatory action and public outcry.

:::

:::eli20
- Facebook gave Cambridge Analytica access to user data
- Data used to manipulate voter behaviour without users' knowledge/consent
- Ethical dilemma: data-driven business model vs safeguarding user privacy
- Outcome: global criticism, legal actions, regulatory changes

:::

## Challenges in Computing Ethics

:::eli10

Computers create problems like: companies watching everything you do online, programs that treat some people unfairly because of their skin colour or gender, fake news that tricks people, and robots that might take people's jobs. These are big problems that need careful thinking.

:::

:::eli15
Major ethical challenges in computing include: mass surveillance and erosion of privacy, algorithmic bias that reinforces discrimination, online harms like cyberbullying and radicalisation, threats to democracy through misinformation, job displacement from automation, and autonomous weapons making life-or-death decisions without human oversight. Each of these requires computing professionals to think beyond just "does the code work?"

:::

:::eli20
- **Privacy & autonomy**: extensive data gathering, surveillance, online manipulation
- **Bias & exclusion**: algorithmic bias mirroring/entrenching societal bias
- **Online harms**: bullying, mental health, radicalisation
- **Undermining democracy**: fake news, micro-targeting, foreign interference
- **Jobs & inequality**: automation displacing workers
- **Automated warfare**: autonomous drones, machines making life/death decisions

:::

## Deliberative Ethical Discussions

:::eli10

When you talk about hard ethical questions with others, there are rules: be respectful, listen to everyone, do not attack people, and use logic instead of shouting. Nobody "wins" the conversation; you all learn together.

:::

:::eli15
Productive ethical discussion requires ground rules: assume good intentions, base arguments on reason rather than emotion, avoid personal attacks or charged language, let each person speak, and accept that the goal is mutual understanding rather than "winning." These deliberative principles help groups work through genuine disagreements constructively.

:::

:::eli20
Rules for productive ethical discussion:
- Everyone is well-intended
- No persuasion without rational substantive basis
- No attacks on individuals
- No charged language, exaggeration, or rhetorical devices
- Each person speaks one at a time
- No winner in a successful deliberative discussion

:::
