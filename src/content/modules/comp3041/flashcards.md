## Flashcards

| # | Question | Answer |
|---|----------|--------|
| 1 | Name the three core ethical frameworks. | Consequentialism (outcomes), Deontology (rules/duties), Virtue Ethics (character) |
| 2 | What is utilitarianism's core principle? | "The greatest good for the greatest number" — maximise net utility |
| 3 | What is Kant's Categorical Imperative? | "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can, at the same time, will that it should become a universal law" |
| 4 | How does virtue ethics differ from deontology? | Virtue ethics asks "what kind of person should I be?" (character); deontology asks "what rules should I follow?" (duty) |
| 5 | What is a logical fallacy? | A defect in an argument where the conclusion is not supported by the propositions |
| 6 | What is a straw man fallacy? | Misrepresenting or exaggerating someone's argument to make it easier to attack |
| 7 | What is ad hominem? | Attacking the person rather than addressing their argument |
| 8 | What is the difference between formal and informal fallacies? | Formal: error in argument structure. Informal: error in argument content. |
| 9 | Name the 8 principles of the SE Code of Ethics. | Public, Client & Employer, Product, Judgment, Management, Profession, Colleagues, Self |
| 10 | What is the difference between certification and licensing? | Certification is voluntary (professional recognition); licensing is mandatory (government permission to practice) |
| 11 | What is Nissenbaum's Contextual Integrity? | Privacy framework: information should flow according to context-specific norms. Violation = flow that breaches norms. |
| 12 | Name the three actors in Contextual Integrity. | Subject (info is about them), Sender (shares info), Recipient (receives info) |
| 13 | What is the "nothing to hide" argument's main flaw? | Mundane info can be weaponised; surveillance has chilling effects; future harm from data collected today |
| 14 | What are the four types of IP protection? | Patents (inventions), Copyright (creative works), Trademarks (brand identity), Trade Secrets (confidential info) |
| 15 | What are the four factors of fair use? | Purpose/character; nature of work; amount used; effect on market |
| 16 | What is copyleft? | Licensing that requires derivative works to remain open source (e.g., GPL) |
| 17 | How does MIT licence differ from GPL? | MIT is permissive (can use in proprietary software); GPL is copyleft (derivatives must also be GPL) |
| 18 | What are Birsch's 5 criteria for accountability? | Causation, Knowledge, Intent, Capacity, Freedom |
| 19 | What is the "Problem of Many Hands"? | When responsibility is distributed across many people, it becomes hard to pinpoint who is accountable |
| 20 | What are the three types of research misconduct (FFP)? | Fabrication (making up data), Falsification (manipulating data), Plagiarism (appropriating without credit) |
| 21 | Name the 6 dependability attributes. | Availability, Reliability, Safety, Confidentiality, Integrity, Maintainability |
| 22 | What caused the Ariane 5 failure? | Float-to-integer conversion exception from reused Ariane 4 code (different rocket, different value ranges) |
| 23 | What is the autonomy-pervasiveness cycle? | Autonomy → cheaper → more pervasive; pervasiveness → more users → justifies more automation |
| 24 | What distinguishes cyber-related from cyber-specific crime? | Cyber-related: computer used but not the primary instrument. Cyber-specific: can only be done through cyber-technology. |
| 25 | What is the GDPR's purpose? | Protecting personal data and privacy rights of EU citizens; regulating collection, storage, and processing by organisations |
