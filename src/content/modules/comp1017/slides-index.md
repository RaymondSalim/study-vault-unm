## Lecture Slides Overview

| Lecture | Topic | Key Concepts | Exam Weight |
|---------|-------|--------------|-------------|
| 1-2 | Sets and Functions | Set notation, operations (∪, ∩, \), Venn diagrams, power sets, Cartesian product, injective/surjective/bijective functions, composition, inverse | 15% |
| 3-4 | Relations | Binary relations, properties (reflexive, symmetric, transitive, antisymmetric), equivalence relations, equivalence classes, partial orders, Hasse diagrams | 10% |
| 5-6 | Logic | Propositional logic, truth tables, logical connectives, predicate logic, quantifiers (∀, ∃), logical equivalence, De Morgan's laws for logic | 20% |
| 7-8 | Proof Techniques | Direct proof, proof by contradiction, proof by contrapositive, mathematical induction (weak and strong), structural induction | (part of 20% logic/proof) |
| 9-10 | Number Theory | Divisibility, primes, Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic, Euclidean algorithm, GCD/LCM, modular arithmetic, congruences | 15% |
| 11-12 | Sequences and Series | Arithmetic sequences, geometric sequences, summation notation, convergence/divergence, recursive definitions, closed forms | 15% |
| 13-14 | Calculus | Limits, differentiation (power/chain/product/quotient rules), integration (substitution, by parts), definite integrals, applications | 15% |

## Notes

- Logic and Proof Techniques together form the most heavily weighted area (~20% combined).
- Calculus questions tend to be the most computationally intensive — practise speed.
- Number Theory problems often combine with proof techniques (e.g., "prove there are infinitely many primes").
- Sets/Functions notation is used throughout; make sure this is solid before moving on.
