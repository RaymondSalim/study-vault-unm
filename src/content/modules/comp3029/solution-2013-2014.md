## Question 1: Segmentation (25 marks)

### (a) K-means Segmentation [7 marks]

**Setup:** An image contains three regions with intensities 30, 70, and 200.

**K-means with k=2:**

1. Initialize two cluster centres (e.g., random or spaced: c1=50, c2=150).
2. Assign each pixel to nearest centre:
   - Pixels with intensity 30 -> c1 (distance 20 vs 120)
   - Pixels with intensity 70 -> c1 (distance 20 vs 80)
   - Pixels with intensity 200 -> c2 (distance 150 vs 50)
3. Recompute centres: c1 = (30+70)/2 = 50, c2 = 200.
4. Converged. Result: Two segments — one merging the dark regions (30 and 70), one for the bright region (200).

**K-means with k=3:**

1. Initialize three centres (e.g., c1=30, c2=100, c3=200).
2. Assign pixels:
   - Intensity 30 -> c1
   - Intensity 70 -> c2 (distance 30 vs 40 vs 130)
   - Intensity 200 -> c3
3. Recompute centres: c1=30, c2=70, c3=200.
4. Converged. Result: Three segments correctly separating all regions.

**Weaknesses of K-means:**

- Requires k to be specified in advance — no automatic way to determine the correct number of segments.
- Sensitive to initialization — different starting centres can lead to different (suboptimal) results.
- Assumes spherical/compact clusters — cannot handle arbitrarily shaped regions.
- Only considers intensity, not spatial coherence — scattered noise pixels may be grouped incorrectly.
- Converges to local minima, not guaranteed global optimum.

**Heuristic for selecting k:**

The "elbow method" — run k-means for k=1,2,3,...,n and plot the total within-cluster sum of squared distances (inertia) against k. The optimal k is at the "elbow" point where increasing k yields diminishing returns in reducing inertia. Alternatively, the silhouette score can be computed for each k, selecting the k that maximises average silhouette width.

### (b) Watershed Algorithm [7 marks]

**Given gradient map (4x4):**

```
1  3  1  3
1  3  1  1
2  2  2  1
1  1  1  1
```

**Watershed Algorithm Steps:**

The watershed algorithm works by "flooding" from local minima in the gradient map, treating gradient values as elevation.

**Step 1: Identify local minima (seeds).**

- Position (0,0)=1: surrounded by values 3,1,3 — local minimum region
- Position (0,2)=1: surrounded by 3,3,1 — local minimum region
- Position (1,0)=1: connected to (0,0) — same basin
- Position (1,2)=1, (1,3)=1: connected region
- Bottom rows contain many 1s forming a connected low region

Local minima at gradient value 1:
- Basin A: {(0,0), (1,0)} — top-left
- Basin B: {(0,2), (1,2), (1,3), (2,3), (3,0), (3,1), (3,2), (3,3)} — these are connected through the bottom

**Step 2: Flood from level 1.**

All pixels with gradient=1 are identified and grouped by connectivity:
- Region A: (0,0), (1,0) — connected
- Region B: (0,2), (1,2), (1,3), (2,3), (3,0), (3,1), (3,2), (3,3) — connected through bottom row

**Step 3: Flood to level 2.**

Pixels with gradient=2: (2,0), (2,1), (2,2)
- (2,0) is adjacent to Region A (via (1,0)) and Region B (via (3,0)) — this is a watershed boundary
- (2,1) is adjacent to Region B (via (3,1)) — absorbed into B
- (2,2) is adjacent to Region B (via (2,3) and (3,2)) — absorbed into B

Since (2,0) touches both basins, it becomes a watershed line. (2,1) and (2,2) are absorbed into B since they only neighbour B at this stage (or become boundary if adjacent to A).

**Step 4: Flood to level 3.**

Pixels with gradient=3: (0,1), (0,3), (1,1)
- (0,1) is adjacent to A (via (0,0)) and B (via (0,2)) — watershed boundary
- (1,1) is adjacent to A (via (1,0)) and B (via (1,2)) — watershed boundary
- (0,3) is adjacent to B (via (1,3)) — absorbed into B

**Final Segmentation:**

```
A  |  B  B
A  |  B  B
~  B  B  B
B  B  B  B
```

Where `|` and `~` represent watershed boundaries (the ridge of high gradient values separating basin A from basin B).

- **Region A:** Top-left (positions (0,0), (1,0))
- **Region B:** Remainder of the image
- **Watershed lines:** Column 1 (top) and position (2,0) forming the dividing ridge

Segmentation serves as a critical component in autonomous vehicle vision systems:

**Challenges:** Real-time processing requirements, varying lighting/weather conditions, and the safety-critical nature of errors (missed pedestrian segmentation could be fatal) make this a demanding application requiring robust, fast segmentation algorithms.

### (e) Graph-Cuts for Segmentation [4 marks]

**Graph-cuts algorithm:**

The image is represented as a graph where:
- Each pixel is a node.
- Edges connect neighbouring pixels, weighted by similarity (low weight = dissimilar = likely boundary).
- Two special terminal nodes (source S and sink T) represent foreground and background.
- Edges from S/T to each pixel encode how likely that pixel belongs to foreground/background (data term).

A minimum cut partitions the graph into two disjoint sets (foreground and background) by severing edges with minimum total weight. This simultaneously minimises boundary cost (cutting dissimilar neighbours) and data cost (assigning pixels to unlikely labels).

**Why cuts need normalization:**

The minimum cut without normalization is biased toward cutting small, isolated sets of nodes. A trivial minimum cut would simply separate a single pixel from the rest of the image (cutting very few edges). This produces degenerate segmentations.

Normalized cuts (Ncut) divide the cut cost by the total association of each partition with the full graph:

$$Ncut(A,B) = \frac{cut(A,B)}{assoc(A,V)} + \frac{cut(A,B)}{assoc(B,V)}$$

This penalises unbalanced partitions and encourages segments that are both internally coherent and reasonably sized, producing perceptually meaningful segmentations.

## Question 2: Motion Analysis (25 marks)

### (a) Vehicle Counting via Frame-Differencing [5 marks]

**Approach:** Subtract consecutive frames to detect moving regions, then segment the difference image (threshold to binary), and count distinct connected components (blobs) as vehicles.

**Three critical issues:**

1. **Vehicle occlusion and merging:** When vehicles travel close together or overlap in the image, frame-differencing may produce a single merged blob for multiple vehicles. Shadow regions between vehicles may also merge detections. Solution: Use morphological operations and size/shape constraints to split merged blobs.

2. **Stopped or slow-moving vehicles:** Frame-differencing only detects motion. A vehicle stopped at a traffic light or moving very slowly produces little or no difference signal and will be missed. Vehicles matching the frame rate of capture may also create ghost artefacts. Solution: Maintain a background model and use background subtraction rather than pure frame-differencing.

3. **Fragmentation of single vehicles:** A vehicle with uniform colour (e.g., a white van) produces difference signals only at its edges (leading and trailing boundaries), potentially fragmenting a single vehicle into multiple blobs. Shadows and reflections can also create additional false blobs. Solution: Use morphological closing to connect fragments, or apply blob size filtering.

### (b) Snooker Ball Tracking: Two Approaches [6 marks]

**Approach 1: Segmentation then Motion Analysis**

*Implementation:* First segment each frame to identify individual balls by colour (using colour-space thresholding in HSV, e.g., red, yellow, green balls against green baize). Then track segmented blobs across frames using correspondence algorithms (nearest-neighbour matching, Hungarian algorithm).

*Pros:*
- Accurate identification of each ball's position per frame.
- Can handle balls that stop moving (still segmented by colour).
- Natural for identifying which specific ball is which (colour labels).

*Cons:*
- Computationally expensive to segment every frame.
- Difficulty when balls occlude each other (touching/overlapping).
- Green balls on green baize are problematic.

**Approach 2: Motion Analysis then Segmentation**

*Implementation:* First compute optical flow or frame differences to identify regions of motion. Then segment only the moving regions to identify individual balls. Static balls are handled by a background model.

*Pros:*
- Computationally cheaper — only processes moving regions.
- Motion detection is straightforward against a static table background.
- Reduces search space for segmentation.

*Cons:*
- Cannot detect stationary balls (a ball that has stopped is invisible to motion analysis).
- Brief contacts between balls create complex motion patterns difficult to separate.
- Fast-moving balls may cause motion blur, making subsequent segmentation harder.

**Conclusion:** For snooker, Approach 1 is generally superior because balls frequently stop, and colour is a strong discriminative cue on the controlled table surface.

### (c) Motion Analysis with Moving Camera [6 marks]

**Problem:** When both camera and objects move, the observed optical flow at each pixel is the sum of:
- Flow due to camera ego-motion (affects all pixels)
- Flow due to independent object motion (affects only object pixels)

Without additional sensors, we must separate these two components from the optical flow field alone.

**Solution using optical flow:**

1. **Compute dense optical flow** across the entire frame (e.g., using Lucas-Kanade or Horn-Schunck).

2. **Estimate dominant motion (camera motion):** The camera's ego-motion induces a consistent global flow pattern across the image. For a translating camera, this is approximately uniform flow; for rotation, it follows a specific parametric model. Fit a global motion model (e.g., affine or projective transformation) to the flow field using robust estimation (RANSAC). The majority of flow vectors correspond to the static background, so the dominant motion model captures camera motion.

3. **Compensate for camera motion:** Subtract the estimated global motion model from the observed flow field. The residual flow represents independent object motion.

4. **Detect moving objects:** Threshold the residual flow magnitude — regions with significant residual flow correspond to independently moving objects.

This approach works because static scene points all move according to the camera motion model, while independently moving objects deviate from this model. RANSAC ensures robustness to outliers (the moving objects themselves).

### (d) Motion Field vs. Optical Flow [4 marks]

**Motion Field:**
- The 2D projection of actual 3D motion vectors of scene points onto the image plane.
- Represents the true physical movement of objects in the scene as observed by the camera.
- A theoretical/physical quantity — what *actually* moves.
- Every scene point that moves in 3D has a corresponding motion field vector.

**Optical Flow:**
- The apparent motion of brightness patterns in the image sequence.
- Computed from image intensity changes over time using the brightness constancy assumption.
- A computational/perceptual quantity — what *appears* to move.
- Estimated from image data using algorithms (Lucas-Kanade, Horn-Schunck).

**Key differences:**
- A uniformly textured spinning sphere has a motion field (all surface points move) but zero optical flow (no brightness change).
- A stationary sphere under a moving light source has zero motion field but non-zero optical flow (brightness patterns shift).
- Optical flow is an approximation to the motion field; they coincide only under specific conditions (Lambertian surfaces, constant illumination, textured surfaces).

### (e) The Aperture Problem [4 marks]

**What it is:**

The aperture problem arises when observing motion through a small local window (aperture). When viewing a straight edge through a limited aperture, only the component of motion perpendicular to the edge can be determined. The component parallel to the edge is ambiguous because the edge looks identical when shifted along its own direction.

For example, a horizontal edge moving diagonally appears (through a small window) to move purely vertically — the horizontal component is invisible.

**Relevance to optical flow:**

Local optical flow methods (e.g., Lucas-Kanade) operate on small image patches. At straight edges or in regions with gradient in only one direction, the flow constraint equation provides only one constraint but there are two unknowns (u, v). The flow component along the edge direction is undetermined. This means local methods can only compute the "normal flow" (perpendicular to the edge), not the full flow vector.

**How to address it:**

1. **Spatial regularisation (Horn-Schunck):** Impose a global smoothness constraint, propagating information from corners and textured regions (where flow is fully determined) to edge regions.
2. **Larger windows (Lucas-Kanade):** Use a larger neighbourhood to capture multiple gradient directions, overdetermining the system (requires texture variation within the window).
3. **Multi-scale approaches:** Combine information across scales to resolve ambiguities.
4. **Feature tracking:** Track corners/interest points where the aperture problem does not exist (gradients in multiple directions provide full constraints).

## Question 3: Tracking (25 marks)

### (a) Template Tracking Failures [4 marks]

**Fixed templates losing track:**

A fixed template is a static appearance model captured at initialisation. It fails when the target's appearance changes over time due to:
- **Pose/viewpoint changes:** A person turning from frontal to profile view no longer matches the frontal template.
- **Scale changes:** Object approaching or receding changes apparent size.
- **Illumination changes:** Lighting variations alter the appearance.
- **Deformation:** Non-rigid objects (people walking) change shape.

The correlation score gradually drops until the template matches background better than the target, causing drift or loss.

**Adaptive templates losing track:**

An adaptive template updates itself each frame to match the current target appearance. It fails due to:
- **Template drift:** Small errors accumulate over time. Each frame's slight misalignment or inclusion of background pixels gets incorporated into the template, gradually corrupting it until it no longer represents the original target.
- **Occlusion adaptation:** If the target becomes partially occluded, the adaptive template incorporates the occluder's appearance. When the target re-emerges, the template has drifted to represent the occluder.
- **Self-reinforcing errors:** Once the template starts drifting toward background or another object, the adaptive update accelerates the drift.

**Trade-off:** Fixed templates are stable but brittle; adaptive templates are flexible but prone to drift. A common solution is to combine both (e.g., weighted mixture of fixed and adaptive components).

### (b) Snakes/Active Contours on Optical Flow Maps [7 marks]

**Concept:** Instead of applying active contours directly to image frames (tracking intensity boundaries), apply them to optical flow magnitude maps where moving vehicles produce high flow values against a near-zero background.

**Implementation:**

1. **Compute optical flow** between consecutive frames using a dense method (e.g., Horn-Schunck). The result is a flow magnitude map where moving vehicles have high values and static background has near-zero values.

2. **Initialise snake contours** around detected vehicle regions (from previous frame's tracked positions or initial detection).

3. **Define energy terms on the flow map:**
   - **Internal energy (smoothness):** Keeps the contour smooth and prevents erratic deformations. Composed of elasticity (prevents stretching) and rigidity (prevents bending).
   - **External energy (image force):** Derived from the flow magnitude gradient. The snake is attracted to strong gradients in the flow map — these correspond to boundaries between moving vehicles and static background.
   - **Constraint energy:** Can encode prior knowledge about vehicle shape (roughly rectangular, minimum/maximum size).

4. **Iterative minimisation:** The snake evolves by minimising total energy, converging to the boundary of the moving vehicle in flow space.

5. **Temporal continuity:** Use the converged contour from frame t as the initialisation for frame t+1, providing natural temporal tracking.

**Advantages for vehicle tracking:**
- Flow maps provide cleaner boundaries than raw intensity (vehicles may be similar colour to road).
- Naturally handles appearance changes (tracking motion, not appearance).
- The snake's smoothness constraints handle noisy flow estimates.
- Provides precise contour of the vehicle, not just a bounding box.

**Challenges:**
- Vehicles that stop produce no flow — snake collapses.
- Multiple vehicles close together may cause snake to merge around a group.
- Optical flow computation is computationally expensive.
- Flow boundaries may be noisy, requiring careful parameter tuning of internal vs. external energy balance.

### (c) Tracker A vs Tracker B in Crowded Scenes [5 marks]

**Tracker A: Strong motion model, weak appearance model**

- The strong motion model can predict where a person will be in the next frame based on velocity, acceleration, and typical pedestrian dynamics.
- In a crowd, motion is relatively predictable (people walk at consistent speeds in consistent directions).
- However, the weak appearance model means that when the prediction places the tracker near multiple similar-looking people, it cannot reliably distinguish the target from others.
- **Expected performance:** Works well when people are spaced apart and moving predictably. Fails during close interactions, crossings, or when two people walk parallel — the tracker may switch to a nearby person (identity swap) because it cannot tell them apart by appearance.

**Tracker B: Weak motion model, strong appearance model**

- The strong appearance model can discriminate the target from other people based on clothing colour, texture, body shape, etc.
- The weak motion model provides poor predictions of position, especially during direction changes or varying speeds.
- **Expected performance:** Better at maintaining identity through crossings and interactions (can re-identify the correct person by appearance). However, during full occlusions where the target is completely hidden, the weak motion model cannot maintain track until the person reappears. Also struggles with sudden motion changes — the search region may not cover where the person actually moved.

**Conclusion:** In a crowded scene, Tracker B (strong appearance) is generally preferable because identity maintenance is the primary challenge. Crowds cause frequent close proximity and crossing, where appearance discrimination is essential. An ideal tracker combines both strong motion and strong appearance models.

### (d) Tracking Challenges [6 marks]

**Motion noise:**
Random, unpredictable variations in a target's motion that deviate from the assumed motion model. Example: A pedestrian being tracked suddenly stops to tie their shoelace, changes direction to avoid an obstacle, or stumbles. The tracker's motion prediction (e.g., constant velocity assumption) becomes inaccurate, potentially causing loss of track.

**Occlusion:**
When the tracked target is partially or fully hidden behind another object. Example: A person walks behind a parked car — for several frames the person is invisible to the camera. The tracker must maintain a prediction of where the person will re-emerge and re-acquire them without drifting to the occluding car. Partial occlusion (e.g., person partially behind a pillar) degrades appearance matching.

**Camouflage:**
When the target's appearance becomes indistinguishable from the background or surrounding objects. Example: A person wearing a green jacket walking past green foliage — the colour-based appearance model cannot distinguish the person from the background. Unlike occlusion (target hidden by foreground object), camouflage means the target is visible but indistinguishable. This defeats appearance-based tracking while motion-based methods may still succeed.

**Object multiplicity:**
When multiple instances of similar-looking objects are present, making it ambiguous which one is the intended target. Example: Tracking a specific white car on a motorway when several white cars of the same model are nearby. After a brief occlusion or crossing, the tracker may latch onto the wrong instance. This is fundamentally an identity management problem, worsened when objects interact or move in formation.

### (e) Kalman Filter Diagram [3 marks]

```
                    KALMAN FILTER CYCLE
                    
    +-----------+                      +-----------+
    |  PREDICT  |--------------------->|  UPDATE   |
    | (Time     |                      | (Measure- |
    |  Update)  |<---------------------|  ment     |
    |           |                      |  Update)  |
    +-----------+                      +-----------+
         |                                   ^
         |                                   |
         v                                   |
    State prediction:                  Measurement z(k)
    x'(k) = A * x(k-1)               from sensor
    P'(k) = A*P(k-1)*A^T + Q              |
         |                                   |
         v                                   |
    Predicted state -----> Kalman Gain K = P'*H^T*(H*P'*H^T + R)^-1
                                             |
                                             v
                                    Corrected state:
                                    x(k) = x'(k) + K*(z(k) - H*x'(k))
                                    P(k) = (I - K*H)*P'(k)
```

**Operation summary:**

1. **Predict:** Use the dynamic model (state transition matrix A) to project the current state estimate and covariance forward in time. Process noise Q accounts for model uncertainty.

2. **Measure:** Obtain a new observation z(k) from the sensor (e.g., detected position of target).

3. **Update:** Compute the Kalman Gain K that optimally weights the prediction vs. measurement based on their respective uncertainties. Correct the predicted state using the innovation (difference between measurement and predicted measurement). Update the covariance to reflect reduced uncertainty.

The cycle repeats each frame, recursively refining the state estimate by balancing prediction confidence against measurement confidence.

## Question 4: Particle Filters, Scene Analysis, HMMs (25 marks)

### (a) Particle Filter Tracking [6 marks]

**General operation:**

A particle filter represents the posterior probability distribution of the target state (position, velocity, scale, etc.) using a set of weighted samples (particles), rather than a single Gaussian (as in Kalman filtering).

**Algorithm steps:**

1. **Initialisation:** Generate N particles randomly around the initial target state. Each particle represents a hypothesis about where the target is. Assign equal weights w_i = 1/N.

2. **Prediction (propagation):** For each particle, apply the motion model with added random noise to propagate it forward in time:
   ```
   x_i(t) = f(x_i(t-1)) + noise
   ```
   This spreads particles according to expected motion patterns, exploring the state space.

3. **Update (weighting):** For each particle, evaluate how well its hypothesised state matches the actual observation (e.g., compare target appearance model with image patch at particle location). Assign weight proportional to the likelihood:
   ```
   w_i = p(z(t) | x_i(t))
   ```
   Particles that explain the observation well get high weights; others get low weights.

4. **Estimation:** The target state estimate is the weighted mean (or mode) of all particles.

5. **Resampling:** Resample N particles from the current set with probability proportional to weights. This eliminates low-weight particles and duplicates high-weight ones, concentrating computational resources on promising hypotheses. After resampling, reset all weights to 1/N.

6. **Repeat** from step 2 for the next frame.

**Key advantages over Kalman filter:**
- Handles non-Gaussian, multimodal distributions (can maintain multiple hypotheses).
- Works with non-linear motion and observation models.
- Can recover from temporary tracking failures if some particles remain near the target.

### (b) ATM Scene HMM [7 marks]

**Scene description:** People approach an ATM, potentially queue, perform a transaction, and walk away. We want to model normal behaviour and detect anomalies.

**HMM Design:**

**States (hidden):** Represent the phases of normal ATM usage:
- S1: **Empty** — no person at ATM area
- S2: **Approaching** — person walking toward ATM
- S3: **Queuing** — person waiting in line
- S4: **Transacting** — person at ATM performing transaction
- S5: **Departing** — person walking away from ATM

**Observations (visible):** Derived from video analysis:
- Motion direction and speed (approaching, stationary, departing)
- Position relative to ATM (far, near, at machine)
- Number of people in scene
- Duration of current activity

**Transition matrix A:** Encodes the probability of moving between states:
```
         S1    S2    S3    S4    S5
S1 [   0.7   0.3   0.0   0.0   0.0  ]
S2 [   0.0   0.5   0.3   0.2   0.0  ]
S3 [   0.0   0.0   0.6   0.4   0.0  ]
S4 [   0.0   0.0   0.0   0.7   0.3  ]
S5 [   0.5   0.0   0.0   0.0   0.5  ]
```

Normal flow: Empty -> Approaching -> (Queuing) -> Transacting -> Departing -> Empty.

**Observation/emission matrix B:** Probability of each observation given each hidden state (e.g., in state Transacting, high probability of observing "stationary at machine" and low probability of "rapid motion").

**Initial state distribution pi:** High probability of starting in Empty state.

**Anomaly detection:**

1. **Train the HMM** on sequences of normal behaviour using the Baum-Welch (EM) algorithm to learn A, B, and pi.

2. **Runtime detection:** For each new observed sequence, compute the likelihood P(O|model) using the Forward algorithm. If the likelihood falls below a threshold, the behaviour is flagged as abnormal.

**Examples of detected anomalies:**
- Person loitering without transacting (stuck in Queuing state too long)
- Person running away after transaction (unusual speed in Departing)
- Person approaching from behind the ATM (unexpected observation in Approaching)
- Reverse sequence (person appears at ATM without approaching)
- Multiple people at the ATM simultaneously during a transaction (shoulder surfing)

### (c) HMM Failure Analysis [6 marks]

**No, we cannot conclude that HMMs are unsuitable based on a single failure.** There are many reasons an HMM might fail that do not reflect inherent unsuitability:

1. **Insufficient training data:** The Baum-Welch algorithm requires adequate training sequences to accurately estimate parameters. With limited data, the learned model may be poor. More data might yield a working model.

2. **Wrong number of states:** The choice of N (number of hidden states) critically affects performance. Too few states cannot capture the complexity of the behaviour; too many overfit to noise. The failure might simply mean the wrong N was chosen.

3. **Poor feature selection:** The observations fed to the HMM may not be discriminative. Changing the feature representation (e.g., from raw position to velocity, or adding appearance features) might solve the problem.

4. **Inappropriate model topology:** A fully connected HMM might be wrong for a sequential process. Using a left-right (Bakis) model or adding skip transitions might be needed.

5. **Local optima in training:** Baum-Welch converges to local optima. Different initialisations may yield better models. Multiple random restarts should be attempted.

6. **Violated assumptions:** First-order Markov assumption (current state depends only on previous state) may be too restrictive. Higher-order HMMs or longer observation windows might help.

**Conclusion:** Before declaring HMMs unsuitable, one must systematically vary: number of states, feature representation, model topology, training data quantity/quality, and initialisation strategy. Only after exhaustive exploration can unsuitability be concluded — and even then, it applies only to that specific problem configuration.

### (d) Parameter Tuning for HMMs [3 marks]

**Parameter tuning** in the context of HMMs refers to the process of selecting and optimising the model's structural and numerical parameters to achieve best performance:

**Structural parameters (set by designer):**
- Number of hidden states N — must be chosen via experimentation (try different values, evaluate on validation data).
- Model topology — fully connected, left-right, or ergodic; which transitions are allowed.
- Observation model type — discrete vs. continuous; number of Gaussian mixture components for continuous observations.

**Numerical parameters (learned or tuned):**
- Transition probabilities A — typically learned via Baum-Welch but initial values affect convergence.
- Emission probabilities B — learned from data.
- Initial state distribution pi.

**Tuning approach:**
- Use cross-validation: split data into training and validation sets.
- Systematically vary structural parameters (grid search over N, topology options).
- For each configuration, train with Baum-Welch (multiple random initialisations) and evaluate on held-out data.
- Select the configuration with best validation performance (e.g., highest classification accuracy or likelihood).
- Regularisation may be needed to prevent overfitting (smoothing of probability estimates).

### (e) HMMs for Complex Motion-Based Behaviours [3 marks]

HMMs are well-suited for recognizing complex motion-based behaviours because:

**Approach:** Decompose a complex behaviour (e.g., "person picking up an object" or "handshake") into a temporal sequence of simpler motion primitives. Each primitive becomes a hidden state, and the observed motion features (optical flow histograms, joint angles, trajectory features) are the emissions.

**Hierarchical HMMs:** For very complex behaviours, use a two-level hierarchy:
- Low-level HMMs recognize atomic actions (reach, grasp, lift, place).
- High-level HMM combines atomic actions into complex activities (making tea = reach + grasp + lift + pour + place).

**Recognition:** Given a new motion sequence, compute the likelihood under each behaviour's trained HMM. The model with highest likelihood identifies the behaviour (maximum likelihood classification). The Viterbi algorithm can additionally recover the most likely state sequence, revealing which phase of the behaviour is currently occurring.

**Strengths for motion:** HMMs naturally model the temporal structure and variable duration of behaviour phases, handle noise in motion observations, and can recognise behaviours performed at different speeds (time warping handled implicitly through self-transitions).
