## Recommended Study Sequence

| # | Topic | Why this order | Estimated time |
|---|-------|---------------|----------------|
| 1 | Functional Basics (Haskell) | Introduces the paradigm shift from imperative thinking; establishes syntax and core concepts | 4 hours |
| 2 | Lists and Recursion (Haskell) | The primary data structure and iteration mechanism in functional programming; heavily tested | 5 hours |
| 3 | Higher-Order Functions | Builds on list recursion; map/filter/fold replace explicit recursion patterns | 4 hours |
| 4 | Types and Type Classes | Deepens understanding of Haskell's type system; makes previous code more rigorous | 3 hours |
| 5 | Prolog Basics | Introduces a completely different paradigm; fresh start with facts, rules, and queries | 4 hours |
| 6 | Prolog Lists and Recursion | Applies recursive thinking in the logic paradigm; parallels Haskell list processing | 4 hours |
| 7 | Paradigm Comparison | Synthesis topic; requires understanding of all paradigms to compare meaningfully | 2 hours |

## Study Tips

- **Haskell first** because the module typically teaches it first, and functional programming builds incrementally.
- **Recursion is key** in both Haskell and Prolog — if you master recursive thinking in Haskell, Prolog recursion becomes much easier.
- **Higher-Order Functions** are the payoff of learning recursion — once you see the patterns, map/filter/fold become natural.
- **Prolog after Haskell** because you can draw parallels (both are declarative), making the transition smoother.
- **Paradigm Comparison last** because you need concrete experience with both paradigms to compare them meaningfully.

## Total Estimated Time: ~26 hours

Spend significant time writing code in both languages. Reading alone is insufficient — you must practise writing functions and tracing execution to build intuition.
