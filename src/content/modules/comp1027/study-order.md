## Recommended Study Sequence

| # | Topic | Why this order | Estimated time |
|---|-------|---------------|----------------|
| 1 | Boolean Logic and Gates | Foundation for all digital circuits; must understand AND/OR/NOT before building anything | 4 hours |
| 2 | Combinational Circuits | Builds directly on gates; adders, multiplexers, and decoders use gate combinations | 4 hours |
| 3 | Sequential Circuits | Adds memory to combinational logic; flip-flops and registers depend on understanding gates first | 4 hours |
| 4 | Computer Architecture | Uses all circuit knowledge to explain how a full CPU is built from components | 5 hours |
| 5 | Machine Code and Assembly | Shows how the architecture actually executes programs; makes architecture concrete | 3 hours |
| 6 | Operating Systems | Explains the software layer that manages the hardware you just learned about | 4 hours |
| 7 | Networking | Most self-contained topic; can be studied independently but benefits from OS context | 3 hours |

## Study Tips

- **Gates first** — everything in digital electronics is built from logic gates; if this is shaky, nothing else will make sense.
- **Combinational before Sequential** — sequential circuits add state/memory on top of combinational logic.
- **Architecture after circuits** — a CPU is just a large collection of the circuits you studied, arranged with purpose.
- **Machine code after architecture** — seeing how instructions flow through the CPU makes fetch-decode-execute real.
- **OS and Networking** are higher-level abstractions and can be studied more independently, but they make more sense after understanding the hardware.

## Total Estimated Time: ~27 hours

Spend extra time practising Boolean simplification and circuit drawing — these are mechanical skills that improve with repetition.
